Restoring after the fuckup

I hope i'm not the only one who completely deleted her $HOME by mistake.
Well, I know I'm not the only one, this is from the UNIX-HATERS Handbook:

Unix aficionados accept occasional file deletion as normal. For example, consider following excerpt from the comp.unix.questions FAQ:

6) How do I “undelete” a file?

Someday, you are going to accidentally type something like:

% rm * .foo

and find you just deleted “*” instead of “*.foo”. Consider it a rite of passage.

Of course, any decent systems administrator should be doing regular backups. Check with your sysadmin to see if a recent backup copy of your file is available.

“A rite of passage”? In no other industry could a manufacturer take such a cavalier attitude toward a faulty product. “But your honor, the exploding gas tank was just a rite of passage.” “Ladies and gentlemen of the jury, we will prove that the damage caused by the failure of the safety catch on our chainsaw was just a rite of passage for its users.” “May it please the court, we will show that getting bilked of their life savings by Mr. Keating was just a rite of passage for those retirees.” Right.

Well, in my case it's a little different. I was doing some cleanup with this handy function I made some time ago:

uprm () { cd .. && rm -rf "$OLDPWD" ; }

Cool, isn't it? What could go wrong with it?
I did ls otherdir and got no result, then I typed uprm thinking I was in otherdir... and of course I got this error message:
Cannot remove /home/izabera : Permission denied
Sudden realization. Horror. Tears.
My IRC friends are still laughing. :(

I just deleted all my local files, my documents, my git trees, my notporn directory... Everything.

Ok, most of the documents are rsynced on my vps too, and I can clone again my git trees from various remotes. I lost my uncommitted work, but that wasn't much anyway.
And this is what uprm looks like right now:

uprm () {  
  if [ "${PWD%/*}" = /home ] ; then  
    echo Nope.  
    return 1  
  else  
    cd .. && rm -rf "$OLDPWD"  
  fi  
}

Thanks to #bash :P
